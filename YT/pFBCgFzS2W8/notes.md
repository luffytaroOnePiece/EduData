# PayPal Architecture: Taming the Thundering Herd

This notebook explores how PayPal’s Braintree team identified and resolved a **Thundering Herd** problem within their Disputes API architecture. The Thundering Herd occurs when a large number of processes or clients concurrently retry failed requests, creating a synchronized "wave" of traffic that repeatedly overwhelms a service, leading to **cascading failures**. In Braintree's case, highly irregular traffic to their Disputes API would cause job queues to build up, trampling downstream processor services. When these jobs failed and retried at fixed intervals, they synchronized with new incoming traffic, creating a cycle of failure that eventually forced messages into a **dead letter queue (DLQ)** for manual intervention.

To resolve this, PayPal implemented a combination of **Exponential Backoff and Jitter**. While exponential backoff increases the delay between retries (e.g., 1, 2, 4, 8 seconds), it alone was insufficient because retries still tended to cluster together and flood the service simultaneously. By adding **Jitter**—a random variation to the retry delay—they were able to **de-synchronize the retry attempts**. This randomness scatters the load across a wider time window, ensuring a more constant rate of requests and preventing the peak loads that cause system crashes.

Beyond algorithmic fixes, PayPal used this incident to **simplify their architecture** by removing unnecessary abstractions. They discovered that a dedicated "processor service" microservice was largely redundant, as its buffering and batching logic could be handled directly by the **worker nodes**. By eliminating this microservice, they reduced system complexity and potential points of failure, adhering to the principle that **simple systems scale** more effectively. The core takeaways for developers are to always use jitter in retry logic and to avoid the "antipattern" of creating microservices for every individual task.
